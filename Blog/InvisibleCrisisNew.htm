One Greenhouse Gas Stands Out

You might wonder why greenhouse gases matter. It is a fair question. Greenhouse gases, as the name implies, are gases that trap heat from the sun. In high concentrations, this can impact the climate by retaining more heat. Water vapor is a natural greenhouse gas. It is not included in climate concerns due to its short life in the atmosphere due to precipitation.

Other gases that can remain for years or more are carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases. However, since the Industrial Age, one greenhouse gas has seen significant increases in the atmosphere. That gas is carbon dioxide. How significant?  Look at the graph below.

So, What Changed?

 

Image by ETA+ via Unsplash

We did. As the Industrial Age brought us advances, we continued to give off increasing levels of carbon dioxide by burning fossil fuels for energy. That trend increased over time. To give you an idea of how much, look at the next graph that shows how much carbon dioxide emissions increased per second over time. The trend has only gotten worse since 2000.

Hold On, Arent Climate Experts Divided?

That is a common myth propagated by corporations and others with a vested interest in maintaining the status quo. A lot of money is made off fossil fuels. In 2016, John Cook and others decided to look at data from several research studies on peer reviewed literature related to climate change. Their work showed a range of 90% to 97% consensus amongst climate experts on human caused climate change. I took their data and aggregated it into the following graph.

Ocean Temperatures Are Rising                                                                                                                   

Image by Joseph Vary via Unsplash

Ocean water absorbs a lot of the heat caused by excess carbon dioxide. You might think that would be a good thing, but it is not. This results in melting polar ice and rising sea levels. We have already seen significant rises in ocean temperature and climate scientists are predicting far worse if we do not reduce carbon dioxide emissions drastically.

With Those Rises in Ocean Temperature Come Rising Water Levels

Coast lines across the world are impacted by rising water levels. Climate scientists predict worsening trends if we continue to emit carbon dioxide at current levels.

Yet many still deny the impact. This photo highlights the irony that some face from that mind set.

Image by Dullhunk via Creative Commons

Lost Real Estate in the United States

There are other signs. Coastal property is already being affected. Rising water levels result in lost coastal habitats and impacts valuable property where many of us vacation. This map shows areas already affected in the United States. Louisiana has experienced many problems due to lost wetlands that are now submerged. This increases the likelihood of damage caused by hurricanes.

Arctic Summers Leave Less Habitat

Image by Annie Spratt via Unsplash

Some areas see the impact sooner than others. Arctic species have bore the brunt of this crisis so far. According to several recent reports, such as one from Nature in 2020, the polar bear may be gone by 2100. The cause often cited is lost summer habitat due to climate change. Many other species are also at risk. The trend downward of Arctic ice coverage shown below looks bleak.
